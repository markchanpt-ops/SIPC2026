Phase 2 — Characterise Communication Patterns
The consultation began with an open, patient-centered invitation, but rapidly transitioned into a highly structured, clinician-led diagnostic interview. Mark consistently used open questions and simple reflections to map out the physical parameters of the pain (the location, timing, aggravating factors, and behavior of stiffness). However, whenever Mdm Lim introduced emotionally significant fears or deep health beliefs regarding "wearing out," "grinding bones," or structural "damage," Mark routinely acknowledged them briefly with a simple restatement before immediately redirecting the conversation back to factual, closed clarification questions (e.g., asking about the number of steps, pain scales, or the flatness of the walking path).
As the interview progressed, this pattern intensified. When the patient shared a highly distressing flare-up experience that anchored her fear-avoidance beliefs, the communication shifted entirely to an interrogation of environmental variables (hills, uneven surfaces) rather than an exploration of her trauma from that event. Finally, when confronted with explicit pushback regarding knee tolerance versus joint degradation, the pattern culminated in an immediate attempt to correct her framework, signaling an underlying push toward clinical education before establishing emotional alignment.
Phase 3 — Interpret the Consultation
This consultation is a classic example of an educator navigating a history using a biomedical data-gathering style masked with basic communication polite phrases.
The consultation evolved from an open discussion about a patient’s anxiety into a highly mechanical interrogation of mechanical symptoms. The underlying communication habit that best explains both the strengths and limitations here is Mark’s tendency to treat patient narratives as checklists of physical diagnostic criteria rather than windows into the patient's cognitive representation of their illness.
Because Mark knows how to ask open questions ("Tell me about...", "What else..."), he successfully uncovers a rich timeline of Mdm Lim's condition. But because his primary goal is to diagnose the structural capabilities of the knee (how many steps can she take, what was the terrain like), he treats her profound fear-avoidance beliefs ("grinding bone down," "something broken inside") as background noise or logical fallacies to be corrected later, rather than the primary barrier to her rehabilitation.
This habit directly explains why he missed the huge functional impact statement regarding her work at the beginning, why he investigated the smoothness of the park connector pavement instead of her fear of re-injury, and why he ultimately tripped over his own "righting reflex" by calling her deep structural anxiety a "common misconception." He prioritized correcting her mechanics before validating her reality.
Phase 4 — Identify Coaching Priorities
1. Prioritizing Belief and Impact Over Somatic Details: Shifting from tracking purely physical variables (pain scores, step counts, terrain) to exploring the functional and psychological impact of those symptoms. If Mark doesn't understand her fear, his mechanical solution won't be adhered to.
2. Validating and Reflecting Health Beliefs Before Educating (Resisting the Righting Reflex): Replacing the habit of labeling patient beliefs as "misconceptions" with deep, complex reflections of their perspective. This preserves rapport and opens a safer space for permission-based education.
3. Using Transitional Summaries to Bridge to Education: Learning to synthesize the patient's subjective experience, functional limits, and fears into a neat summary before introducing clinical concepts.
Phase 5 — Coach the Student
Coaching Feedback
Hi Mark,
You did an excellent job establishing an open, welcoming environment at the start of this session. You naturally use conversational open prompts like "tell me more about what has been bothering you" and "Tell me about what you feel once you wake up," which allowed Mdm Lim to share a clear timeline of her knee stiffness and pain. You also used a wonderful complex reflection when she spoke about her pain lingering ("I understand how that might make you feel like its doing more damage to the knee"). That was a brilliant moment of genuine empathy that connected her physical symptoms to her internal anxiety.
However, as the conversation progressed, an underlying habit started to limit your effectiveness: you tended to focus heavily on checking off mechanical and environmental details at the expense of exploring her deep fears and beliefs.
When Mdm Lim dropped major clues about how this is affecting her life—mentioning early on that it is "really affecting my work" and later that she feels like she's "grinding the bone down"—your immediate instinct was to zoom in on physical data collection. You asked for pain scales, the exact number of stairs, and whether the park connector path had hills or uneven surfaces.
Because you were focusing on the physical mechanics of her walk, you missed the emotional weight of what she was telling you. When a patient is terrified that exercise is actively destroying their joint, asking them if the ground was flat feels like an interrogation of the environment rather than support for their distress. This culminated in you calling her perspective a "common misconception" right before asking to educate her. Even though you politely asked for permission, labeling her fear as a mistake put her on the defensive, causing her to close down and respond with, "to be honest, it's very hard for me to understand."
To help Mdm Lim safely start moving, she needs to feel that you completely understand her fear of breaking her knee.
Your Next Step to Practise
In your next session, consciously resist the "righting reflex" when a patient shares an incorrect medical belief. Instead of correcting it or pivoting to environmental details, use a complex reflection that mirrors their exact worry back to them, and back it up with an affirmation of their caution.
* What it sounds like: If a patient says, "I feel like exercise is just wearing the joint down faster," instead of explaining tolerance or terrain, try saying: "You're deeply worried that moving this knee is going to permanently damage it, and because you care about your health, you're trying to protect it from getting worse."
Once the patient nods and feels completely heard, only then ask for permission to share a different perspective. Try this on your next case, and notice how much more receptive the patient becomes to your advice.
